How VoltSol’s SEO Strategy Actually Works

A plain-language walk-through of how the website is built to win Google searches, what Google sees and stores, and how a real homeowner ends up clicking through and becoming a lead. No jargon — or jargon explained when we have to use it.

Last updated June 17, 2026 · Reflects the live site (200 city pages, 28 guides, full structured data)
0

The 30-second version

Google is a matchmaker. When someone types “off grid solar Sacramento” into Google, Google’s job is to instantly hand them the most relevant, trustworthy pages it knows about. Our entire strategy is about making VoltSol’s website the obvious answer for thousands of solar-related searches across California — so the homeowner clicks us, not a competitor.

We do that three ways: (1) build a page for almost every relevant search someone might make, (2) make each page genuinely useful and unique so Google trusts it, and (3) hand Google the business’s real-world credentials so it treats us as a legitimate local company. Everything below is detail on those three moves.

The core idea
We are not buying ads. This is organic search — the free results below the ads. It compounds: once a page ranks, it brings in leads month after month without paying per click. It takes longer to build than ads, but it doesn’t stop when you stop paying.
1

How Google works (in three steps)

Behind the scenes, Google does three distinct things. Understanding them is the key to everything else.

1
Crawling
Google runs automated robots (called “crawlers” or “spiders”; the main one is “Googlebot”) that follow links around the internet, page to page, reading everything they find. They discover new pages by following links from pages they already know — and from a special file we hand them called a sitemap (more below).
2
Indexing
When the crawler reads a page, Google stores a copy and analyzes it: what is this about? What questions does it answer? Is it high-quality or thin junk? It files all of that into a massive library called the “index.” If a page isn’t in the index, it can never show up in search results — it effectively doesn’t exist to Google.
3
Ranking
When someone searches, Google pulls every indexed page that could answer the query and puts them in order — best answer first. Hundreds of signals decide that order: relevance, quality, trust, how local the business is, page speed, and more. Position #1 gets the vast majority of clicks; almost nobody scrolls to page two.
Why this matters for us
Our strategy maps cleanly onto these three steps: we make sure every page gets crawled (sitemap + links), gets indexed (unique, quality content), and ranks well (relevance + trust + local authority).

The site structure — a pyramid of authority

We didn’t just build a homepage and a contact form. The site is built as a deliberate hierarchy — a pyramid — that Google rewards because it shows topical depth and organization.

Tier 1
Homepage /
The brand hub. Tells Google who VoltSol is, what it sells, and where it operates. Carries the business credentials.
Tier 2
State hub /market/solar/california
The “money” authority page for California solar — links down to every county.
Tier 3
County hubs (24) /…/[county]
One per county. Carries county-level facts: local utility rates, permit office, climate zone. Links to its cities.
Tier 4
City pages (200) /…/[county]/[city]
The workhorses. One page per city — the page that ranks when someone searches “solar in [their town].”
Content
The Learn hub (28 guides) /learn
Educational articles that catch people earlier in their research — before they’re even ready to buy.

Every tier links down to the tier below and links back up. That internal linking does two jobs: it lets Google’s crawler reach every page easily, and it passes “authority” around the site so the whole thing ranks stronger than a flat pile of disconnected pages would.

Why 200 separate city pages?
Because people search locally. Someone in Fresno searches “solar Fresno,” not “solar California.” A single generic page can’t rank for 200 different towns — but 200 dedicated, locally-specific pages can each rank for their own town. This is how you capture the entire state instead of a sliver.
3

What gets indexed — and what stays hidden

Not everything on the site is meant for Google. We deliberately control what gets indexed.

✅ Indexed (we want these found)
  • Homepage
  • State + 24 county hubs
  • All 200 city pages
  • The Learn hub + all 28 guides
  • Technology, pricing, estimate, booking pages
🚫 Blocked (kept private)
  • Everything under /admin (this doc included)
  • The lead database + dashboard
  • /api/ internal endpoints
  • Customer booking tokens & personal data

We control this with two tools working together:

1
robots.txt
A plain text file at voltsolenergy.com/robots.txt that politely tells crawlers “you may read everything except /admin and /api.” Well-behaved bots (Google, Bing) obey it.
2
The sitemap
A machine-readable list at voltsolenergy.com/sitemap.xml of every page we want indexed — currently 260 URLs. It’s like handing the librarian a complete catalog instead of making them wander the stacks. It also tells Google when each page last changed, so it knows what to re-check.
Belt and suspenders on privacy
This very document lives under /admin, so it is (a) blocked in robots.txt, (b) behind the admin login, and (c) tagged noindex so even if a bot somehow reached it, it’s told not to store it. Three independent locks.

Why each page actually ranks — uniqueness + trust

Building 200 pages is easy. Building 200 pages that Google respects is the hard part — and where most competitors fail.

The doorway-page trap (and how we avoid it)

If you make 200 near-identical pages where only the town name is swapped, Google calls these “doorway pages” and penalizes them — sometimes the whole site. It’s considered spam.

So every one of our city pages carries genuinely different, true information about that specific town:

  • The actual local electric utility and its rate (e.g. PG&E ~$0.44/kWh vs. SMUD ~$0.18 vs. SDG&E ~$0.47). (PG&E = Pacific Gas & Electric; SMUD = Sacramento Municipal Utility District; SDG&E = San Diego Gas & Electric; kWh = kilowatt-hour, the unit your bill is measured in.)
  • That county’s real permit office and typical turnaround
  • The local climate zone and realistic sun-hours
  • Three unique, town-specific FAQs (frequently asked questions)
  • Local notes (coastal fog, desert heat, mountain snow load, etc.)

That variation is what makes each page a legitimate, useful answer instead of spam. It’s the difference between dominating and getting penalized.

Structured data — speaking Google’s native language

Buried invisibly in each page’s code is a section of “structured data” (also called “schema”). Humans never see it, but it spells out facts in a format Google reads perfectly: this is a local business, here’s its phone, its license number #1148585, its service area, its prices, its reviews.

This is what can earn the fancy search results — the star ratings, the FAQ drop-downs, the business info panel. We’ve wired all of it: the local-business markup, services, FAQ markup, articles, the CSLB (Contractors State License Board) license, and the review/rating markup.

One honest caveat
The star-rating code is in place, but Google only displays stars sourced from real Google reviews. That’s why getting actual customer reviews is the one piece we can’t code our way around — it needs real customers.

The user journey — from Google search to lead

Here is the whole thing from the homeowner’s side, start to finish:

  1. 1
    They search
    A homeowner in Roseville with a scary PG&E bill types “off grid solar roseville” or “how to avoid PG&E rate increases” into Google.
  2. 2
    Google matches
    Google checks its index and finds our Roseville city page (for the first search) or our PG&E-rates guide (for the second) — both built specifically to answer that exact query.
  3. 3
    They see a rich result
    Our listing appears with a compelling title (“Off-Grid Solar in Roseville from $8,700”), a useful description, and — as reviews accumulate — star ratings and FAQ drop-downs that make it stand out from plain-text competitors.
  4. 4
    They click through
    They land on a page that speaks directly to their town: their utility, their permit process, their sun exposure. It feels local and credible, not generic.
  5. 5
    They convert
    The page guides them to the free-estimate form. They fill it out. That lead lands in the admin dashboard — scored hot/standard/low — and an email alert fires to info@. Hugo follows up.
The Learn hub catches them earlier
Not everyone is ready to buy. The 28 guides catch people in research mode (“is off-grid solar worth it?”), build trust, and link them toward the city pages and estimate form when they’re ready. It widens the top of the funnel.

Two halves: what we built vs. what’s next

SEO has two halves. We’ve completed the first; the second needs real-world action.

On-site — ✅ DONE (the code)
  • Site structure & 200 city pages
  • 28 educational guides
  • All structured data / schema
  • Sitemap, robots, fast page speed
  • Titles, descriptions, internal links
Off-site — 🔜 NEXT (needs Hugo)
  • Google Business Profile (claim + verify)
  • Real customer reviews
  • Directory listings / citations
  • Submit sitemap to Google & Bing
  • Backlinks from other reputable sites

Think of the on-site work as building a great store on the best corner in town. The off-site work is getting listed in the phone book, collecting customer reviews in the window, and getting the local paper to mention you. Google weighs both — and for a local business, the off-site trust signals are enormous.

The single highest-leverage next step
Claim and verify the Google Business Profile (GBP) — the free business listing that puts VoltSol on Google Search and Maps. It powers the map pack (the top-3 local results with the map), feeds the star ratings, and is the #1 trust signal for local search. The verification postcard takes up to two weeks, so the clock starts the day Hugo requests it. The full copy-paste pack is already prepared.

What to expect — the honest timeline

Organic SEO is a compounding asset, not an instant switch. Realistic expectations:

  • Weeks 1–4: Google discovers and indexes the new pages. Business Profile verification happens. Early long-tail searches start trickling in.
  • Months 2–4: Pages climb as Google gains trust. Reviews and citations accumulate. The map pack starts appearing for local searches.
  • Months 4–9: Authority compounds. City pages rank for competitive terms. Leads grow steadily without per-click cost.
The bottom line
The expensive, technical foundation is built and live. From here the growth comes from consistency — reviews, citations, and patience — and it pays back every month for years, unlike ads that stop the moment you stop paying.
