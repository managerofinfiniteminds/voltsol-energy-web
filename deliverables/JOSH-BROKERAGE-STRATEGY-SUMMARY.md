# The Brokerage Model — Strategy Summary
**Source: call with Josh Orozco, Thu 2026-07-02 ~9:00 PM PDT | Distilled 2026-07-03**

---

## The one-line version

Instead of selling a *lead*, sell an already-**closed, signed contract** — as a tradeable financial instrument — to whichever licensed installer pays the most for it.

## The problem it solves

In the current solar sales model, a rep closes a deal and then waits weeks to months to get paid commission, contingent on the installer actually funding, permitting, and installing the job. That lag is what burns reps out and pushes good door-knockers out of the industry. Meanwhile installers who *do* want volume have to manage a sales force, commission structures, and all the "drama" that comes with 1099 reps.

## The mechanism

1. **Brokerage contract, not a standard install contract.** A sales rep or company holding a broker's license signs a legally binding contract directly with the homeowner — full specifics locked in: equipment, gross contract price, expected net profit, financing/PPA structure (using Aurora or similar proposal tools to lock the numbers).
2. **The signed contract becomes paper.** It has a known gross value and a known expected margin the moment it's signed — a real financial asset, not a lead.
3. **Sell the paper.** The broker sells that signed contract — outright, or auction-style to the highest bidder — to a licensed installer. Example: gross contract $60,000, expected net profit $15,000, sold today for $5,000 cash.
4. **The installer takes it from there.** They attach the brokerage contract as an amendment to their own installer contract, handle funding, and either send their own W-2 rep to close the paperwork in person or send a paid-hourly (not commission) closer. The installer's own CSLB license handles all fulfillment, permitting, and install — the broker's name never touches the job.
5. **Reps get paid immediately.** Instead of $15-30K in commission paid out over 3+ months contingent on install completion, the rep/broker gets a smaller amount (e.g. $5K) in cash within days. Faster, smaller, certain — versus slower, bigger, contingent.

## Why Josh thinks it works

- **Modeled on the alarm industry** — contracts get sold and monetized immediately rather than paid out over a long install/service tail.
- **Removes rep-vs-installer friction on both sides.** Installers get a ready-to-install, no-drama contract with no sales reps to manage. Reps get paid same-week instead of waiting on an installer's funding and install timeline.
- **A market structure, not a single company.** Josh's original vision is a website — "Uber for solar contracts" — where installers log in, pick a zip code and price range, and bid on a queue of signed contracts. Reps (an app similar to Uber's driver app) could accept available deals to close in their area, pass a qualification bar (years in industry, deals closed, quizzes), and get a flat stipend per converted lead (e.g. $1,000, paid next-day) instead of commission.
- **Overhead elimination.** Everything is cash, paid immediately, no long AR tail. The broker's core function is arbitrage between "what a signed deal is worth to a reseller" and "what it cost to close it."

## What's confirmed vs. what's speculative

**What's real today:** the underlying mechanism — brokerage contract structures exist in solar/home-improvement licensing today (Josh describes it as a section/amendment on top of a standard contractor's license, not a novel legal invention) — and Josh has already sketched out a website/app concept for it.

**What's speculative:** whether this scales into a real marketplace, what the actual legal exposure is state-by-state (broker licensing requirements, CSLB rules, disclosure obligations to the homeowner about who will actually perform the install), what volume of installers would actually bid on unseen contracts sight-unseen, and whether $5K per contract is a sustainable price point once more brokers enter the market and compete the margin down.

## How this connects to VoltSol's existing leads-business thesis

This is a further-downstream extension of the leads-business vision (`LEADGEN-BUSINESS-VISION.md`), logged there as **Phase 3.5 — sell the closed deal, not just the lead**. Where the core thesis sells a *qualified lead* to a licensed pro pulled from a public registry, this model goes one step further and sells an *already-closed, signed contract* — a different, heavier regulatory lane (broker/contractor-license structure, not just data brokering) that would need real legal review before it becomes more than a thesis.

It is **not a replacement** for the lead-marketplace plan — it's a second, later-stage product for a business that's already good at capturing and qualifying leads (Phases 1-3), applied one level further down the funnel where a deal is fully signed rather than just introduced.
